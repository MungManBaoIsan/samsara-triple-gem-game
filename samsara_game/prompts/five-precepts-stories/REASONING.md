# Reasoning: Five Precepts Stories

This document captures the thinking behind the prompt. It exists so a reader can understand not just *what* the prompt is, but *why* it ended up this way.

## Goal

Add five new NPCs — one for each of the Buddhist Five Precepts (non-killing, non-stealing, sexual ethics, no intoxicants, truthful speech) — whose stories would teach the ethical foundation of Buddhist practice through emotionally immersive narrative. The constraint was to make the content real enough for adults to relate to while remaining appropriate for children who might also play the game.

## Iteration history

This was a single prompt listing all five precepts explicitly. The approach agreed on earlier in the project — showing emotional and mental states rather than graphic scenes — carried forward here. The precepts became five new characters (Jamie, Riley, Sam, Alex, Taylor) each placed across different realms based on the type of suffering their precept involves.

The hardest precept to get right was the third precept (sexual misconduct): the story of Sam, who cheated on a partner, needed to convey betrayal of trust, loss, and the possibility of repair without being explicit or inappropriate for younger players. The solution was to frame it entirely through the emotional experience — the breaking of trust, the honest apology, and the act of helping others — rather than through any physical description.

Earlier in the project, the decision had been made to use abstraction: rather than depicting drug abuse as a scene, show the mental state of craving that underlies it. The same principle applied here: Jamie doesn't witness a violent death — he finds a crumpled butterfly in his hand. Alex doesn't describe getting drunk in detail — he says "I could have killed someone." The visceral detail is emotional, not graphic.

## Failure modes the final version handles

- **Preachiness:** Generic moral lessons for children often feel like rules from above. The five characters avoid this by having specific, memorable details that make the precepts feel lived rather than prescribed. "I can still see its wings crumpled in my hand" sticks with a player in a way that "killing is wrong" never would.
- **No redemption:** A story that only shows suffering without a path forward would leave younger players without hope. Each NPC has a clear change arc that ends with the character helping others — modelling the precept in action.
- **Adult content creep:** The third precept especially required careful phrasing. Framing the story around betrayal of trust (rather than the physical act) made it accessible across age groups while still being emotionally honest.

## Outcome

Five fully developed NPCs with 3-dialogue transformation arcs each, distributed across Hell, Hungry Ghost, Animal, and Human realms. All five pass the dual test of being relatable to adults (real consequences, genuine guilt) and accessible to children (no graphic content, clear emotional language, positive resolution). The game now teaches both the Three Poisons (from earlier NPCs) and the Five Precepts as complementary ethical frameworks.

## What I'd change next

The five precept NPCs were added after the realm design was complete, so their placement across realms was somewhat ad hoc. A cleaner design would map each precept to its natural realm from the start — stealing to the Hungry Ghost realm (craving), killing to the Hell realm (hatred), intoxicants to the Animal realm (ignorance/clouded mind) — so the cosmological placement reinforces the teaching through the map itself, not just through dialogue.
